☢ Radioactive Dating and Half-Lives ☢

By Hudson Huckeba, Emmalin Clevenger, and Willa (Yitsira) Springer

Definition

Radioactive dating is a scientific technique used to determine the age of materials — such as rocks, fossils, or archaeological artifacts — by measuring the amount of a naturally occurring radioactive isotope and its decay products.

A half-life is the fixed amount of time it takes for half of the atoms in a sample of a radioactive isotope to decay into a different element or isotope. This decay occurs at a predictable rate and is unaffected by temperature, pressure, or chemical environment.

Scientists use these principles to date objects ranging from ancient fossils to the Earth itself.

Description

Radioactive dating relies on the principle that certain isotopes are unstable and decay over time into stable isotopes. By measuring the ratio of the original (parent) isotope to the decay product (daughter isotope), scientists can calculate how many half-lives have passed, which translates into the age of the sample.

For example, carbon-14 dating is commonly used to date organic materials like bone or wood, while uranium-238 dating is used for much older rocks because of its extremely long half-life (4.5 billion years). This allows scientists to construct accurate timelines of Earth's history, evolution, and the development of life.

The method is incredibly precise because the decay rates of isotopes are constant and predictable. It also allows for cross-verification using different isotopes, providing confidence in age estimates.

Radioactive dating has numerous applications, including archaeology, geology, paleontology, and even planetary science. It is an essential tool for understanding the timing of events in Earth's history and for studying ancient human artifacts.

Examples

Carbon-14 half-life: 5730 years

Uranium-238 half-life: 4.5 billion years

Americium-245: 740 milliseconds

Neptunium-240: 3 seconds

Francium-223: 22 minutes

Radon-222: 3.82 days

Cobalt-60: 5.27 years

Plutonium-239: 5,730 years

Uranium-235: 24,110 years

Potassium-40: 1,248,000,000 years
